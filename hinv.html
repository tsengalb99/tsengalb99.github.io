While at Caltech, I started collecting vintage computers, and started the Caltech Vintage Computing Club. I still collect vintage computers, and below is an inventory (hence the page being called hinv) of my collection. I either still have these items or left them with the club. Last updated April 2021.

Parallel computers

Caltech/JPL Mark II Hypercube - A board from the Mk II 386 based hypercube.

Caltech/JPL Mark III Hypercube - A board from the Mk III M68k based hypercube.

Honeywell WARP board - My dad's, from his PhD.

*NIX workstations

SGI O2 (1996) & Personal Iris 4D/25 (1989) - These machines were used by the Goddard lab at Caltech, presumably not for compute purposes since neither the O2 or Personal Iris were particularly fast when new. The O2 has a R10000 and 128MB of memory and the 4D/25 has a R3000 and 24MB, nothing too crazy.

HP 9000 735/125 (1992) - Also used by the Goddard lab, with 400MB(!) of memory in 1992. Runs HP-UX. I tried running NeXTSTEP on it at one point but since NeXTSTEP uses addresses above 64MB for video RAM (thus not liking 400MB of RAM) that wasn't much fun. 640K 64MB ought to be enough for anyone.

IBM RS/6000, SUN SparcStation 4/40, SGI Indy, DEC AlphaStation 4/233 (early 90s) - Things I haven't gotten around to turning on.

Dell T7910 (not vintage, unless you're FaceGoogSoft) - What this site used to be hosted on. 2x E5-2667v3, 256GB RAM, 4TB disk, 2x RTX2070 + GTX1070. Not bad for a machine mostly built out of random parts I found in e-waste. Technically a *NIX workstation since it runs Ubuntu.

PowerMac G5 (2000s, not vintage but entirely obsolete) - Technically a UNIX workstation too. Worked great as a space heater when my heater in Avery died.

Interesting peripherals

Immersadesk R2 (1994) - A "portable" CAVE VR system (it's the same size as a Smart Car and weighs 500 pounds). Was used by the Caltech CMS department and Jim Barry, and has a SIGGRAPH '99 sticker on it. The case has sufficiently warped so that it no longer fits through a standard 34" door, making moving it around quite annoying.

IBM 129 Keypunch (1974) - Was left in an ethernet closet in Beckman Institute for 30+ years until I acquired it. Gave it a CLA and now it works perfectly. Somehow, I didn't win "most useless hack" at Hacktech with a Hollerith to Google Cloud API interface, which I'm still slightly annoyed by. Also great for add cards at Caltech.

IBM Selectrics (1960s-80s) - A blue Selectric II, 2 tan Selectric II's, a tan Selectric III, and a single dial Selectric Composer which is currently disassembled on my workbench. I have a feeling I'm going to fail miserably at getting it back together.

VT220 (1980s) - Served as a text conky for the T7910. The keyboard controller kept throwing errors, preventing it from being used as an actual serial terminal, but LK201 is so bad that I wouldn't want to use it anyways.

Original LaserJet (1984) - A laser printer, from 1984. Pretty cool stuff for the 80s.

HP 7550A Line Plotter (1980s) - A desktop line plotter. Made a map of Caltech with it. Apparently the pen accelerates at 10G's (!). Had to use melted ballpoint pens since all the pens that came with it were dry.

IBM T220 "Big Bertha" (2001) - A dual domain IPS 4K 16:10 monitor, from 2001. Cost as much as nice car in 2001, probably worth as much as that car right now. Requires 4 DVI inputs to run 4K at a whopping 43Hz.

Mouse Systems M-2 mouse (1980s) - An optical mouse, from the 1980s! Requires its own custom mousepad.

PCs & Macs

Intel (dual?) 486 Deskside (1990) - Massive Intel deskside AT server with 2 486DX50's (or so I'm told, I've only loaded DOS on it which doesn't support multiple CPU's so I haven't verified this), 24MB (on ISA expansion cards), 1MB Tseng ET4000, and 512MB disk through a CF2IDE adapter. Supposedly was an Intel internal test bench machine in the 90s, now it's my DOOM machine.

Gateway 2000 486DX66 (early 90s) - Not sure what the Goddard lab was doing with a 486 machine.

Corona Data Systems PC Clone (early 80s) - Got it for free with 2 Selectrics. Don't have an EGA monitor, but it turns on.

OEM 386 PC (late 80s) - Came with a CRT and light pen (!). Boots into DOS, which claims it has 1MB of RAM.

Zenith Z180 Laptop (1985) - Belonged to the late Kim Border from 1985 to 2019. Missing a power supply so I've never turned it on.

Osborne-1 (1981) - One of the first portable PCs. Runs CP/M, came with a BASIC floppy.

Mac SE (1986) - An early SE, with the signature case. Was used by the math department.

Mac LC, Twentieth Anniversary Macintosh (1990s) - Never turned them on.

Random items and not-so-interesting things

A box of magnetic core memory and vacuum tube (1950s, 60s) - From an alum who is now probably in his 90s.

Western Electric 1500 phones (1960s-80s) - Probably the only person to have had a working fax machine and three landlines in their dorm in the 21st century. Made for a great alarm clock and doorbell though.

HDS Viewstation (late 80s / early 90s) - An actual X terminal!

Various mechanical keyboards - Seems to be all the rage these days to have a keyboard that annoys your neighbors.

Various CRTs - Random CRTs, except for a Sony 2K CRT. The first 2K monitor I ever had.

DEC Rack, RL02, and Front Loading Tape Drive (1970s/80s) - Everything but the Girl PDP-11. Apparently you can interface a RL02 with USB, to get a massive 10MB USB drive.
